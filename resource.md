Foundational Numeracy(pg 84):
Foundational Numeracy means the ability to reason and to apply simple numerical concepts in daily life problem solving. When children acquire the following skills, it is said that they have developed number sense and spatial understanding.
 It includes the ability to:
i. Make an understanding of quantities
ii. Develop concepts like more and less, and larger and smaller
iii. Establish relationships between single items and groups of items (seven means one group of seven items which is one more than a group of six items).
iv. Use symbols that represent quantities (7 means the same thing as seven).
v. Compare numbers (10 is greater than 8, and three is half of six).
vi. Arrange numbers in a list in order: 1st, 2nd, 3rd, etc.
vii. Visualise shapes and space around them



Children grow up counting their toys, toffees, people at home or other small sets of objects. They are often asked who has more/less or are there enough objects in their regular conversations. So, when schools begin to develop the understanding of pre-number concept, they should build upon the child’s experience from his/her familiar context.
 
Classification involves putting together things that have some characteristics in common. So, when organising tasks on classification, we must make sure that the activities are meaningful to them and, they are familiar with the objects which she/he mustclassify.Initiallychildrenshouldbeencouragedtoclassifyonone property/characteristic only. Gradually, complexity of the task should be increased where they can classify on more than one property such as colour, size, shape etc. This will later help them in understanding number sense.

Seriation involves ordering a set of objects according to some rule. Intrinsically, it also involves ordering objects in two directions. For example, the child applies the relations ‘bigger than’ and ‘smaller than’ at the same time. It also means understanding the logic of transitivity which means that if A is more than B and B is more than C, then A is also more than C. Seriation also forms the base for understanding of patterns. Thus, it should be build using the objects from the familiar contexts of children and initially using 3 objects only. Gradually, children should be presented with more objects to seriate.

One-to-one correspondence involves matching or pairing of objects. For building upon the understanding of one-to-one correspondence, children need to understand the meaning of ‘many and few’, ‘more than/ less than’ and ‘as many as’. Teachers need to design tasks contextual to child’s context so that the child relates and uses them in daily life experiences.

Child Psychology Points in NISHTHA FLN
1. Brain Development & Neuroscience
Over 85% of a child's cumulative brain development occurs before age 6 [making early years the most critical window for care and stimulation.
The brain is most flexible and adaptable to learning during early childhood.
Rapid brain development in early years means children need a nutrient-dense diet; deficiencies in protein, vitamins, and minerals can cause delayed mental and motor development with lasting effects.
Positive early experiences support lifelong learning and development; learning gaps, if unfilled early, continue to widen.

2. Cognitive Development
Children vary in cognitive abilities and styles — they think, analyse, and make decisions differently.
Cognitive development includes classification, seriation, matching, sorting, sequential thinking, and one-to-one correspondence.
Children move from perception-bound thinking to concept-based understanding through direct experience with physical, social, and natural environments.
Ex. - A child may memorize “3 + 2 = 5,” but true numeracy develops when the child can actually see, touch, compare, group, and use quantities in real life .
Instead of directly directly giving 4>2 give 4 stone and 2 stone 
Mathematical thinking (quantitative and spatial reasoning) is a key subdomain of cognitive development, built through meaningful activities.
Ex.- Arranging five objects on the basis of their size.( Make the question like that student have ability of mathematical thinking )
Information processing — children visualise text and create mental images to comprehend it (schema-based memory).
If a child imagines:
 🍎🍎🍎 + 🍎🍎
           they mentally visualize quantity before solving:
             3 + 2 = 5
           This improves number sense and problem-solving.
Age and psychological maturity directly affect reading ability.
            A 3-year-old child may enjoy pictures and listening to stories
             A 6-year-old child may  identify letter ,read simple words
Multilingualism is linked positively to cognitive flexibility and scholastic achievement.

3. Socio-Emotional Development
Early childhood is described as a critical period for social and emotional development (equated with mental health).
ex.-A calm child can carefully count objects and identify mistakes more easily.
Socio-emotional development includes: experience and management of emotions, ability to form positive relationships, and ability to explore the environment.
Positive socio-emotional development builds self-confidence, empathy, meaningful friendships, and a sense of personal value.
Socio-emotional development influences all other areas of development.
The quality of early experiences lays either a strong or weak foundation for how children respond to the world for the rest of their lives.
Children need to develop pro-social behaviours: caring, sharing, helping, cooperating.

4. Self-Concept & Self-Regulation
Children need to develop a positive self-concept — they must be encouraged and admired so their self-image grows healthily.
Self-esteem develops through autonomy, confidence in growing abilities, and achievements nurtured from early childhood.
Self-regulation — the ability to focus, refocus, and manage distractions -is identified as a critical competency. Yoga, meditation, visualisation, and breathing techniques are recommended as age-old practices to develop it.
Using a language children don't understand can damage their identity, self-confidence, and self-esteem, leading to negative academic outcomes.

5. Motivation & Learning Approaches
There is a strong research link between a child's interest and their motivation, achievement, productivity, and perseverance.
Intrinsic motivation is activated when classroom content connects to children's personal interests and goals.
Children should be encouraged to become inquisitive, initiative-taking, confident, innovative, and reflective learners.
Ex.- A child is learning counting and says:
“1, 2, 3, 5…”
            instead of feeling embarrassed, the teacher responds:
  “Very good! You counted carefully till 3. Let’s find the next number together.
Confidence is particularly important for fearlessness and happiness in learning.

6. Learning Styles & Individual Differences
Children have different modality preferences: visual, auditory, kinaesthetic, or tactual — the majority prefer visual learning.
Example:
🍎🍎🍎 + 🍎🍎 is easier than only “3 + 2”. (visual learning improves number understanding)
jumping while counting,arranging blocks,sorting object -> improves mathematical concept
Rhymes and songs help children remember number sequences ,tables      patterns and counting order
Environmental preferences (time of day, temperature, seating) also affect learning performance.
Grouping preferences — some children work best alone, others prefer collaborative settings.
Competency-based education matches instruction to the child's developmental readiness, not age or time — children move forward only upon mastery.

7. Play & Learning
Play is the primary mode of learning in early childhood — it supports physical, emotional, cognitive, and linguistic development simultaneously.
Through play children develop social and emotional skills, self-control (waiting for turns, following rules), and problem-solving.
Pretend play and role play develop imagination and social understanding.
Curiosity, initiative, and perseverance develop through exploratory and discovery-based play.

8. Social Learning & Relationships
Children learn social skills by watching, mimicking, and interacting with adults and peers.
Parents and caregivers have the biggest role in social/emotional development as they offer the most consistent relationships.
Consistent relationships with family, teachers, and adults help children understand emotions in predictable interactions.
Learning is described as an active, collaborative, and social process — interaction between teacher–child, material–child, and child–child is essential.
Children internalise activities, habits, behaviours, and ideas from their social context (aligned with Vygotskian social learning principles, though not named explicitly).

9. Secure Attachment & Early Environment
Children who have secure attachments, safety, good nutrition, and enriched learning opportunities in early years are better prepared for school.
Children from less fortunate early environments show differences in school readiness.
A warm, nurturing, safe environment is essential — children must feel relaxed and connected with peers.
Children need to be protected from emotional and psychological distress, and taught about good touch/bad touch.

10. Fear, Anxiety & Stress in Learning
A fear-free classroom environment is repeatedly emphasised — fear hinders participation, confidence, and expression.
Mathematics anxiety is explicitly mentioned and should be avoided through peer learning, collaborative problem-solving, and teacher support.
Self-assessment is preferred over third-party assessment as it avoids the child feeling labelled or stressed.
Children should learn in a stress-free atmosphere where they can express thoughts and feelings freely.

11. Language, Identity & Psychology
A child's home/mother tongue is tied to their identity and emotional wellbeing — teaching in an unfamiliar language leads to feelings of helplessness and low self-esteem.
Mixed language use (translanguaging) supports meaning-making, understanding, and identity affirmation.
A fear-free linguistic environment is needed — children should never be pressured to learn a second language too quickly.

Yes/No Questions are Easy :
 According to child development theories (e.g., Jean Piaget):
Children only need to recognize information, not generate an answer.
They require less working memory and cognitive effort.
They reduce language load, helping children focus on the mathematical concept.
Young children feel more confident because they have only two response options.
      Demerit: A child can guess correctly with a 50% chance. 
In child psychology, Yes/No questions are not very effective for determining a child's behavior, thinking process, or mathematical understanding. They mainly show whether a child can recognize something.

Why Comparison as the Foundation of Numeracy
Jean Piaget, in The Child's Conception of Number (1941), showed that children's relationship with numbers starts with one simple skill: comparison. Before a child can count, order, or conserve quantity, they can already tell which pile has more, which glass has less, or when two things look the same. Every higher numerical skill — sequencing, counting, understanding that quantity doesn't change when you rearrange objects — builds on this basic ability to compare.
This is why comparison-based tasks are the right starting point for any foundational numeracy intervention.
